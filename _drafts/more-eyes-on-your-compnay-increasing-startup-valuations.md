When my old team and I invested in public markets, we generally looked at about 100 stocks for every investment we made. We've found similar trends among other asset managers. For us, that means analyzing often hundreds of pages of public company information before even looking at a stock price chart. Before any trading, the big question was always " is this a good firm? "

I asked that San Francisco founder how they'd feel about making public online their basic business information and financial statements. They told us that they'd be more comfortable posting limited information on Crunchbase. More specifically, he told us they used the allure of financial details, business specifics, and operational practices as a sort of bait for qualified investors. Certainly there exists some merit to the idea. But our fundamental question to him was simple:

Would more eyes on your company increase its potential valuation?

[https://www.sec.gov/files/2022-oasb-annual-report.pdf](https://www.sec.gov/files/2022-oasb-annual-report.pdf)

Thing is, per that same SEC study, the distance between a lead angel investor and their target company averages only 37 miles. 🤯 If you can only fundraise in your own backyard, then naturally only a limited number of investors can even consider your company. And with only a small number of perspectices, you could really be passing up the chance for an investor that truly believes in your product, service, or mission. Anectdotally, these passionate early investors that " click " with founders are the most helpful down the road.

How do we increase your offering's diversity and, perhaps most importantly for increasing business value, reach? Could the answer lie in a more public disclosure of your company vision? I'll lay out some rules of thumb for this later in the post, but I want to tell you one more story before we dive into the schemantics of having a great private placement. Feel free to skip ahead if you just want the facts.

![image](https://www.gstatic.com/images/branding/product/2x/avatar_anonymous_white_48dp.png)

Only few investors get access and therefore to get to price the company, as opposed to the hundreds of thousands of investors who might have priced the company differently if they had access in a more open market

## The Takeaway

What follows are some best practices we've picked up over the years through our own efforts and those of small-business clients. They are based around our public disclosure platform. Issuers.info was one of the first things we built at Block Transfer because transaprent company disclosures—one of our core values—are not very well disseminated today. In fact, your normal stockbroker has details very similar to those hosted on our platform. But that information isn't generally publically accessable, leading many investors to turn to outside data sources like Yahoo Finance or Bloomberg. We appreciate the former, but the data it leverages often comes from select media outlets rather than the actual underlying company. And you shouldn't need to pay $25,000 a month to get that data from the latter. Of course, if you're like most startups, these two aren't even relevant.
