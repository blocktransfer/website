This post lays out some more of my background and business perspective to start [our series](https://www.blocktransfer.com/blog/post/private-placement-fundraising-39-secrets) on building your own successful venture. 👋 Just over 7 years ago, I started researching financial markets after working a (great) minimum-wage job because I was tired of working open to close alone. Since then, I quit my one and only corporate gig to trade stocks full-time. I was hooked after making more in two weeks (on paper) than the entire year prior. It lit a spark inside that lead me to sleep for two hours a day for over half a year at Georgia Tech, just to best trade currency futures.

Along the way, I uncovered the revolutionary potential of blockchain technology through the world of cryptocurrency investments. After I lost $100,000 I'd generated solely from investment returns, I knew there had to be something special about these new distributed ledgers. That's when I founded my first blockchain startup, MonerAds, foraying itno the statup world.

## Collaboration from Day One

From sales and marketing to technical development skills, MonerAds was my "real world" MBA in how to (not) launch a compnay. We never spoke to a single end-consumer (not one!) from ograization inception to eventual retirement. With no mentors to guide me then, I turned to free online resources for advice. Although little of what was avaliable back then helped us succeed, this appraoch did deeply embed in me an appreciation for [distributed free quality education](https://www.wootenwealth.com) . That's why I'm writing our latest blog series for new business founders.

### Constant Personal Help

Early on, I remember asking my high-school math teacher to explain some wierd aspect of the obscure Mimblewimble cryptographic protocol (yes, I handed him the actual whitepaper). Since then, I've stumped a number of tutors asking about wierd cryptography. Although many of these advisors didn't exactly understand our business, their perspectives were instrumental in crafting our service descriptions and theoretical value proposition.

On that note, after a 3-page formal incporation, I teamed up with a talented frontend developer I'd knwon since middle school. Importantly, this was a collaboration of convenience---specifically mine. I mocked up some [template employement contracts](https://drive.blocktransfer.com/external/58e890662c2f947cf14f76c8e4b8426cc1654799aa06480cee27b14f587a0c0a) and we agreed to split future revenues. Just like that our new tech startup was born.

### Product-Focused Vision

Our idea was to help online newspapers losing money because of ad blockers. While some major sites at the time would display a popup asking you to disable your ad blocker, none of them had a true way to monetize these readers outside of subscriptions. We saw an opportunity as avid adblock users ourselves in an age before adblock detectors and the hard paywalls seen today. We racked our brains together to come up with a way that any reader could contribute to the site, even if they got annoyed by obtrusive ads.

An innovative new cypto at the time called Monero was our answer. Due to cool math things, Monero is/was (haven't been following recent developments) super efficient to "mine" on standard computers, laptoips, or phones. That means you can create a relatively meaninful amount of the cryptocurrency by just running its blockchain algorithms on your computer.

Over a couple years after school, we basically built a system where you could run a Monero miner directly in your browser (it was a very crude implementation looking back). Then our idea was that you could have this popup on the articles:

### Secret #4: Outsource Early and Often

If you read that last sales letter then you'd notice how much I talked about myself rather than actual product benefits, client needs, etc. But aside from poor form, you'd also see a whole section talking about how I spent months diving into IRS publications, corporate tax codes, and comprehensive accounting practices. This information certainly helped me down the road founding a transfer agent, but it was a rediculous use of resources at the time. As a [broke](https://youtu.be/rkB0BbIWG9k) high school student, I didn't think I could hire an accountant or talk to any professionals. That was a naive and scarse viewpoint that I think ultimately led the company to statis, whereby I wouldn't hire anybody to do important development work that needed more R & D efforts. Rather, I took the DIY approach and tried to figure everythng out myself---resulting in tremendous knowledge very useful now; but [zero revenue](https://drive.blocktransfer.com/external/584c3050d633ceded232d34d414759ccb22ea9cb652466c5b8f8d216e2bfe275), publisher users, or media attention.

We never ended up raising funds, and the business ultimately ended up dying out. But, as I tell the story recently as an ecample, some investors still think the idea has merit.
