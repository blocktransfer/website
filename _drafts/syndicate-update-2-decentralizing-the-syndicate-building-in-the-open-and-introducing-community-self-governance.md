## Decentralized Work

Decentralized work is permissionless work. It works because anyone, anywhere, anytime can choose of their own volition to embark down a career path simply because it intruiges them. I know from experience that the best trades often come on a whim. They’re a combination of preparation, subconcious pattern recognition, and a little bit of luck. None of that works when you need to pass through five rounds of interviews to write your first line of code.

When I wrote Nine to Noon, I just decided one day to start typing words in a free document editor. Every source I used was freely avaliable online without any kind of special account. And every hour I invested trading to learn the market reacted to more-or-less the same financial statistics avaliable to anyone else with a Yahoo Finance bookmark.

When we lower the bar ot participate in the economy, we open our organziations up to the innovative ideas of hidden genuises capable of so much more than command-and-control forced labor. (ONE_WORD_IDEALLY) We need those permissionless systems for the next generation of asset managers, software developers, and free thinkers. For the sake of progress itself, I posit that society ought best advance rapidly under these principles.

### Conway’s Law

What I am proposing is a new kind of work that hasn’t been deployed at scale yet. I believe deeply that the organization of workers naturally becomes apparent in final products. Might we consider the best structure for creating an equal, transparent, and efficient market for capital?

We know the existing financial instutions rely on centralized work schemes. They have their “pyramid” with workers on the bottom and a select few executives at the top. I wholeheartedly believe this thought style led to the problematic present chain of custody:

If we believe in an ideal future where everyone owns their own keys to their securities, many of these intermediaries cease relevance. Their purposes, while historically ustified, can get automated in the context of modern blockchains. If we agree that a market with less middlemn, gatekeepers, ad beurocrats best serves investors, regulators, and issuers; then it follows that we need a decentralized market.

A decentralized market gives the power back to users of the systme, not its maintainers. It captures value for these users in toto, not a select few middlemen scraping off [processing fees](HREF_JEFFRIES_PERCNET_FEE_TWEET). And by its distirbuted nature, this new market requires a different approach to work.

Over the past seven years uncoving the blockchain landscape, I’ve seen this new approach flourish. I’ve heard and seen thousands of developers self-organize online through thier own research to uncover a project. They pour thousands of hours into bettering these systems, sometimes without a cent of pay or even an identifying name.

If this unique collaboration structure can produce an industry worth XYZ trillion dollars, almost entirely informally & mdash;do you think there’s much more potential hiding latent? Presently, almost the only way to develop these web3 systems is to follow Wall Street’s lead. You learn the market, understand the code, and go through some kind of politicking to land pay at a centralized company.

There are some projects that try to “solve” this porblem, aiming for a more decentralized workforce. But then these projects themselves are a central group of select actors. The parallel would be us trying to fix Wall Street by opening a centralized ATS.

Indeed, while these blockchains have succeeded using token incentives alone in these formative years, uncapped growth isn’t a long-term sustainable work incentive. This is why modern ealry hires in tech hubs still want good ‘ol greenbacks. Stock options alone simply can’t pay rent, buy groceries, or cover childcare.

The question for me always comes back to asset management. How do you allocate pay without a central abriter of salaries? I believe the answer lies in the crowdsourcing ethos underlying the industry.

### New Technologies

Most of what I know about programming came from the introduction to Python call at Georgia Tech. At least foundationally, this was my big moment of understanding how computers process logic. I remember learning about `for` loops in class one week and building an EDGAR scrapign bot with them the next.

APIs in particular were such a transformative concept to learn about as a massive numbers geek. I warm up inside just at the thought of basic price action data feeds. Once I understood that I could apply coding to the markets, I was off to the races to build this key tooling as fast as possible.

Early on, I remember the joy of running very basic scripts through Repl.it and seeing my results immediately. It was an addictive high until it wasn’t, and that generally happened when I got dictated a lackluster homework assignment. Then it was stress and headaches until I could get back to the stock market.

As I learned more about Stellar over the years reading the docs, open-source code, and public discussions, I realized my present vision for our markets. Then, on XOZY_DATE_FIRST_PY_TAD3_COMMIT, I started [building it](commit hash githubHREF). For XYZ_18_ABC months, I kept iterating in the public light, as the codebase was open but not free.

The community gave me some stellar feedback on my ideas, and they were sincerely welcoming towards my work. But outside of periodic technical questions, it was self-paced independent work any moment I could find some spare time. It was this repo where I developed many of the infrastructural choices underlying the Syndicate by the nature of encountering them in actual build design considerations.

I believe this is a unique modern approach to building things that wasn’t possible before the widespread use of public code repositories. Given the length of time it probably took Nakamoto, they couldn’t build like this in the open back then. Indeed, the “definative” central whitepaper and initial deployment of Bitcoin reflect the isolated, painstaking, and siloed developmemt patterns of traditional organizations.

Part of the quadary is a fear of theft. What if someone copyed an early version of Nakamoto’s work? By keeping the draft stages private, we can ignore this risk.

Bitcoin might have succeeded, but what about the tens or even hundreds of similar concepts that died in the dark? Condemned to the lonely pages of an academic journal, an abandoned hard drive, or even an unfinished private git repo. I believe that the best scalable efficincies may have emmerged earlier if everyone had insights into these comparisons.

In high school, I had an idea for compensating writers without selling ads, which I kept top-secret. I remember reluctantly spilling out the basic concept for MonerAds on a date, only after extensive pleading. Over time, I shared the idea with my friends more and more, until the point where I was talking about it publicly and on YouTube.

Despite all this sharing, nobody copied the concept. Nobody ever asked to even look over the nonexistent code. In fact, I actually attracted collaborators that helped me flesh the concept out.

Ultimately, we uncovered an established firm building the tech. Combined with the challenges of mining efficinecies in volatile markets, that business ceased operations. But its demise was quite explicitly not the result of copycats because ideas are a dime a dozen, and it all comes down to execution.
