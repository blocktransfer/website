## Complete Control of American Investment Markets

Most of the shares are held in street name at DTC and they have expanded their role to the point that they decide who gets to be a public company and who doesn't.

If an issuer cannot get DTC eligibility they cannot trade their security on the open market. This often leads to investors losing their entire investment due to a lack of liquidity.

Remember how the major stock exchanges own a big slice of the DTCC? Well, in 2006, they got the SEC to [require all their companies](https://www.sec.gov/files/rules/sro/nyse/2006/34-54289.pdf) to be "[DTC Eligible](https://www.dtcc.com/settlement-and-asset-services/underwriting/new-issue-eligibility) ."

## The "SRO" Sham

Issuers need to be able to access the public marketplace and the decision should not be monopolized by a private company that sets its own guidelines without oversite or any appeal process.

With the move toward street name ownership and lack of access to most of the information

held at DTC, clearing and brokerage firms, it has become very difficult to determine

what is really happening with the trading of a security.
Now, let's add another layer of complexity: the OBO/NOBO rule. Brokers have the option to classify your account as either an "objecting beneficial owner" or "non-objecting beneficial owner." OBO is the default setting, which means you're anonymous to the companies you're investing in. It can be hard to change to a NOBO account. This anonymity creates barriers to effective corporate governance.

Street-name ownership means all the voting materials, proxy cards, and even dividends go through financial middlemen before reaching you.

Imagine you're at a concert, and you've got three types of tickets:

1. Direct Registration via Transfer Agent : gives you VIP access to the front row,

1. Street-Name NOBO : lets you walk into the middle bleachers with extensive security, and

1. Street-Name OBO : requires you to go through a series of confusing hallways and middlemen before you can even see the stage.

Owning stock through a broker is like being stuck in the back row of the concert, straining to hear the music over the chatter of the crowd.

In our concert analogy, think of NOBOs (Non-Objecting Beneficial Owners) as the VIP ticket holders. They're upfront and personal, willing to engage directly with the band (the company). The band knows who they are, can send them special offers, and can even invite them backstage for a meet-and-greet. There's a direct line of communication, and it's clear who's who.

On the flip side, OBOs (Objecting Beneficial Owners) are like the general admission crowd standing in the back, faces hidden in the dim light. They're there for the music but don't want any direct interaction with the band. The band knows someone is out there listening, but they have no idea who. Now, here's the kicker: the band has to pay a premium to a middleman just to send a shoutout or a song dedication to this anonymous crowd.

This anonymity, symbolized by the OBO status, not only creates barriers to effective corporate governance but also adds layers of unnecessary costs. Just like how the band has to pay extra to interact with the general admission crowd, companies have to shell out more to reach their OBO shareholders. And who's profiting from this setup? The middlemen, who are incentivized to keep this convoluted system going because they can charge exorbitant fees.

(Good luck becoming a NOBO at some brokers.)

### The Hidden Costs of Anonymity: How Brokers Profit at the Expense of Companies and Shareholders

The OBO/NOBO rule doesn't just create a barrier to effective corporate governance; it also has a financial impact that many investors are unaware of. When you're an OBO, brokers can essentially charge companies whatever they want to distribute proxy materials to you. This has led to a perverse incentive system where brokers are motivated to send as much material as possible, just so they can bill the company more. It's like a concert where the middlemen, not the musicians or the fans, are setting the ticket prices and taking a large cut.

This practice has even led to a bizarre bidding war among physical material distributors. These distributors will bid up the price they're willing to pay a broker for the "privilege" of sending out proxy materials. Why? Because they know they can pass those costs onto the companies. It's a major source of revenue for brokers, but it's a cost that companies have to bear, often without fully understanding the extent of these charges.

Imagine going to a concert and finding out that not only did you pay for your ticket, but the band also had to pay exorbitant fees to multiple middlemen just for the "privilege" of playing to you. It would make the whole experience feel like a rip-off, right?

This system is not just inefficient; it's fundamentally broken. It distorts the true cost of shareholder engagement and puts a financial strain on companies, which ultimately affects their performance and, by extension, shareholder value.

Our blockchain-based solution aims to cut out these costly middlemen, bringing transparency and fairness back to the proxy voting process. By doing so, we're not just making the system more efficient; we're making it more equitable. It's time to take the power away from the brokers who have been profiting from this broken system and put it back where it belongs: with the companies and their shareholders.

OBOs are those who wish to keep their identity hidden, while NOBOs are open to being contacted by the company whose shares they own. Sounds simple enough, right? But here's the catch: most people don't even know this choice exists.

By default, many brokerage accounts set shareholders as OBOs, preserving their anonymity. While this might sound like a good thing, it creates a significant barrier between the company and its shareholders. Companies often have to pay exorbitant fees to industry monopolies just to get a list of NOBOs, who are the only shareholders they can directly contact. It's like having to pay a premium just to find out who's actually in the audience at the concert, while the majority remain hidden in the shadows, their voices unheard.

This default setting of anonymity not only hampers effective corporate governance but also adds unnecessary costs to the process. Companies are willing to pay these high fees because direct communication with shareholders is crucial for informed voting and governance. But should they have to?

Our blockchain-based solution aims to cut through this Gordian knot. By providing a transparent yet secure platform, we make the OBO/NOBO choice explicit and easy to change. Shareholders can toggle their preference with a simple click, and companies can access this data without having to go through costly intermediaries. It's like bringing everyone out of the shadows and into the front row, creating a more direct and democratic shareholder experience.
