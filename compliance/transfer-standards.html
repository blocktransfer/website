A blockchain is an open, distributed ledger that records transactions between two parties in a verifiable and permanent way using cryptography. Transactions on the blockchain are verified and authenticated by computers on the network (referred to as "nodes" or "validators") that receive, propagate, verify, and execute transactions. The process of authenticating a transaction before it is recorded ensures that only valid and authorized transactions are permanently recorded on the blockchain in collections of transactions called "blocks." Blockchain networks are based upon software source code that establishes and governs their respective cryptographic systems for verifying transactions. The recording of Issuer Assets on the blockchain will not fundamentally affect the valuations of issuer securities. Just because issuers use us does not suggest that issuers will invest in any cryptocurrencies (referred to as, among other things, virtual currencies), and any other such interpretation is construed and unruly.

Upon the creation of an account, shareholders will create a blockchain wallet. Each investor will be assigned a unique Stellar network blockchain address for their wallet, and they will be able to track the balance of Issuer Assets in their wallet on a public "block explorer" tool capable of displaying activity on the Stellar network. Users of the Stellar network must pay transaction fees (in the form of "Stellar Lumens," the native digital asset for the operation of Stellar) to the Stellar network in order to validate a transaction. Such transaction fees are intended to protect the Stellar network from frivolous or malicious computational tasks. Investors will not be required to purchase any Stellar Lumens. Upon registration, we provide all accounts with sufficient Stellar Lumens for ordinary interaction with Issuer Assets. Should more funds be needed, they will be provided on a case-by-case basis at our sole discretion.

Delays in transaction processing have occurred on the Stellar network. Such a delay may occur on account of, among other things, the inability of nodes to reach consensus on transactions. During a network delay, it will not be possible to record transactions in the shares on the blockchain. Should such a delay occur for an extended period of time, we could choose to effect transactions with shareholders manually (i.e., in book-entry form only) until such time as the network has resumed normal operation, whereby we will be bound to standard three-day turnaround rules pursuant SEC Rule 17Ad-2.

In the future, Issuer Assets may be maintained and recorded on a blockchain other than the Stellar network if we determines that such a change would be in the best interest of issuers and shareholders. Furthermore, securities may be available for purchase, sale, or transfer from one shareholder to another shareholder (or potential shareholder) ("peer-to-peer") on the blockchain, including through the SDEX or any other location in which issuer securities are registered to transact.

Risks Applicable to Digital Securities

Block Transfer maintains direct registration records of security holders using digital securities accounted for on the Stellar blockchain network through controllable electronic records, which may pose unique risks to book-entry investors.

Controllable electronic records have the same rights, preferences, and privileges as traditional securities of the same class, but settle differently than traditional securities. Controllable electronic records are uncertificated, registered securities, the ownership and transfer of which are recorded on a blockchain ledger that will be publicly distributed. We recognize ledger transfers on the blockchain as material fact on issuer's capital books.

Generally, distributed ledger technology involves multiple copies of a synchronized electronic database, or distributed ledger, that are separately maintained by various participants on a network. Complex cryptographic processes are employed across the distributed ledger network as the means by which participants on the network achieve a consensus as to which transactions are valid, and therefore, should be immutably recorded within the relevant distributed ledger.

The validity of publicly available copies of the Stellar blockchain ledger can be mathematically proven utilizing cryptographically-secured distributed ledger network technology. The cryptographically-secured distributed ledger network technology employed with respect to controllable electronic records could be technology that is currently used for trading digital currencies (e.g., the Bitcoin blockchain), technology similar to that used for trading digital currencies that is adapted for trading controllable electronic records, or novel technology designed principally for transactions in controllable electronic records.

Our digital securities are traded an open trading system with limited volume and liquidity.

Because digital securities settle differently from traditional securities, such as shares of our issuers' common stocks traded on the national market system ("NMS"), our book-entry controllable electronic records are not be listed for trading on any stock exchange or through any other NMS trading platform. Our controllable electronic records are issued on a blockchain network that is not registered with the SEC and transacted through holder-posted orders generated using cryptography. Moreover, only holders with access to a blockchain wallet, such as one provided by us, will be able to buy and sell our controllable electronic records on the Stellar blockchain. Any disruption to the operations of the the Stellar blockchain would materially disrupt trading in, or potentially result in a complete halt in the trading of, our controllable electronic records.

Holders may place offers to buy or sell securities through the SDEX, a free functionality of the Stellar blockchain network. Trades of controllable electronic records do not require the involvement of a central depositary, broker, ATS, or Block Transfer. Rather, controllable electronic records will be directly held and traded by their direct-registered owners on the Stellar ledger, which will be publicly published. The transaction costs associated with this process relate to the de minimis costs of Stellar network transactions conducted by holders in the event of transfer, private sale, or the like. As a result, Block Transfer—rather than clients or holders of our controllable electronic records—will bear such minimal costs required in connection with embedding cryptographic hash functions in the Stellar blockchain. The validity of publicly available copies of the Stellar ledger can then be mathematically proven utilizing cryptographically-secured distributed ledger network technology.

Because our controllable electronic records will be traded exclusively on a blockchain trading system, it is likely that there will be a limited number of holders of controllable electronic records. As a result, this novel trading system may have limited liquidity, resulting in a lower or higher price or greater volatility than would be the case with greater liquidity. You may not be able to resell your controllable electronic records on a timely basis or at all.

The use of distributed ledger technology is novel and untested with respect to controllable electronic records.

Our controllable electronic records trade on a novel, untested system developed by the Stellar Development Foundation for trading digital assets.

This novel trading system is subject to all the usual risks associated with the fact it is new and untested, including:

  • a rapidly-evolving regulatory landscape, which might include security, privacy or other regulatory concerns that could require Block Transfer to implement changes to its controllable electronic records trading system that could disrupt trading in our controllable electronic records;
  • the possibility of undiscovered technical flaws in the Stellar blockchain network, including in the process by which transactions are recorded or by which the validity of a copy of the global ledger can be mathematically proven utilizing cryptographically-secured distributed ledger network technology;
  • the possibility that cryptographic security measures that authenticate transactions and the blockchain for the applicable distributed ledger could be compromised, which could allow an attacker to alter the distributed ledger and thereby disrupt the ability to corroborate definitive transactions; and
  • the possibility of breakdowns and trading halts as a result of flaws in the Stellar blockchain network that could prevent trades for a period of time.
Distributed ledgers we use are publicly available, which may give rise to privacy concerns.

Copies of the distributed ledger will be available to the public and will store the complete trading history from inception of the issuance of each series of controllable electronic records. As a result, robust and transparent trading data, other than holder identity, with respect to our controllable electronic records will be publicly available via the published proprietary and distributed ledgers. The controllable electronic records trading data will be secured by cryptographic key pairs and only the public-key-derived wallet address will be exposed to the public on the distributed ledger. The personal identity information necessary to associate a public key representing a given block of digital securities with the owner of those digital securities will be maintained in a separate database that is not exposed to the public. If there are security breaches with respect to the holders' personal identity information database resulting in theft of the information necessary to link personal identity with public keys, the stolen information could be used to determine the affected holder's complete trading history in our controllable electronic records. Moreover, concerns over these privacy issues may limit adoption of this novel trading system by a range of potential investors, reducing liquidity of our controllable electronic records.

The Stellar network will automatically publish to the internet information necessary to prove the validity of any book-entry transaction of controllable electronic records. This process ensures that anyone with basic cryptographic technical skills will have access on a near real-time basis to the embedded data necessary to prove the validity of any publicly available copy of the Stellar ledger. As a result, there will be robust and transparent trading data (specifically, the number of securities traded by each anonymized account, the price of each trade, and the balance of the securities held in each anonymized account) available to the general public. In addition, Block Transfer matches holders to transactions recorded on the Stellar blockchain ledger. As a result, Block Transfer has all information necessary to complete clients' capital books and records with respect to each controllable electronic record transaction (specifically, the identity of each digital security's owner, the number of securities traded in each transaction, the price of each trade and the balance of securities held by each owner).

The private cryptographic keys associated with controllable electronic records could be stolen.

Our controllable electronic records are represented by balances written on the Stellar blockchain and secured by cryptographic associated key pairs. The associated private key is necessary to effect the sale of a given block of our digital securities and, as such, is meant to be kept private. The general public, however, is not yet accustomed to using secure cryptographic methods and managing private keys.

The number of controllable electronic records traded on the Stellar blockchain may be very small, making the market price more easily manipulated.

While Block Transfer has adopted policies and procedures such that digital security holders are not free to manipulate the trading price of controllable electronic records contrary to applicable law, and while the risk of market manipulation exists in connection with the trading of any securities, the risk may be greater for our controllable electronic records because the SDEX is a new system that does not have the same breadth of market and liquidity as the NMS. There can be no assurance that the efforts of Block Transfer will be sufficient to prevent such market manipulation. For example, there can be no assurance that multiple securityholders will not be able to manipulate the stock price by opening multiple accounts and trading amongst themselves.

Transactions in our controllable electronic records are recorded on the Stellar blockchain ledger, which will be electronically published on a real time basis. The blockchain ledger maintained by the Stellar network reflects the definitive ownership record with respect to our controllable electronic records. The validity of publicly available copies of the Stellar ledger can be mathematically proven utilizing cryptographically-secured distributed ledger network technology. If, for any reason, the distributed ledger technology utilized with respect to a series of our controllable electronic records were to become unavailable and suitable alternative technology were not available to be implemented, investors in such controllable electronic records would not have a means of proving the validity of publicly-available copies of the proprietary ledger. As a result, the accuracy of publicly-available trading information could be called into question and investors may elect to no longer transact in controllable electronic records.

We are not a stock exchange and has no listing requirements for issuers or for the securities recorded.

Unlike NMS trading platforms, there are no minimum price or other listing requirements for controllable electronic records to be traded on the Stellar blockchain. As a result, trades of our controllable electronic records on the Stellar blockchain may not be at prices that represent the national best bid or offer prices of securities that could be considered similar securities.

The price of our controllable electronic records, even when we issue equity controllable electronic records that rank on a parity with our clients' common stock, is not pegged to the value of our clients' common stock.

Fungible controllable electronic records can be converted or exchanged for traditional securities of the same class trading on the NMS. Notwithstanding, controllable electronic records may have significantly different pricing associated with the different trading environment for controllable electronic records as compared to securities trading the NMS. The following factors may drive such a pricing differential between our securities trading on the NMS and our equivalent book-entry controllable electronic records transacted between holders using the SDEX:

  • delays and fees associated with transfer from "street name" to direct registration;
  • lower volume and liquidity of the digital securities on a closed trading system;
  • immediate settlement of the digital securities compared to traditional T+2 settlement; and
  • novelty of the controllable electronic record trading platform.
